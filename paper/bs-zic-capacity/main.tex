\markboth{The Capacity Region of the Binary Symmetric Z-Interference Channel}%
{The Capacity Region of the Binary Symmetric Z-Interference Channel}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We give a complete, self-contained closed-form characterization of the
capacity region of the two-user binary symmetric Z-interference channel
$Y_1=X_1\oplus X_2\oplus N_1$, $Y_2=X_2\oplus N_2$, with independent noises
$N_i\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p_i)$, $p_1,p_2\in(0,\tfrac12)$, for all parameters.
When the interfered receiver is the less noisy one ($p_1\le p_2$), the region
is the polytope $\{R_1{+}R_2\le 1-h(p_1),\ R_2\le 1-h(p_2)\}$; in the weak
regime ($p_1>p_2$) the region coincides with that of the degraded binary
symmetric broadcast channel with parameters $(p_1,p_2)$, treating
interference as noise with an optimized input bias is capacity-achieving,
rate splitting is unnecessary, and the sum capacity $1-h(p_2)$ is attained
only with the interfering user silent. The weak-regime converse is an
eight-line application of Mrs.~Gerber's Lemma, and achievability follows
from a two-line algebraic identity. The ingredients are classical --- the
strong regime instantiates strong-interference machinery, and the weak
regime is capacity-region equivalent to Benzel's discrete additive degraded
interference channel --- but we are not aware of the closed form for all
parameters, or of this short a derivation, appearing in one place.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{I}{nterference}, not noise, is the binding constraint in
multiuser wireless systems, and the two-user interference channel is its
canonical model. The \emph{one-sided} (or Z-) interference channel, in which
only one receiver suffers interference, is the minimal such model: it
isolates a single interference link, and every phenomenon of interference
management --- rate splitting, joint decoding, treating interference as
noise (TIN) --- already appears in it. For the \emph{Gaussian} Z-interference
channel the capacity region in the weak-interference regime remains a
long-standing open problem despite decades of effort~\cite{Costa85}. It is
therefore natural to ask for the simplest \emph{noisy, discrete} instance:
the binary symmetric Z-interference channel (BS-ZIC),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:model}
Y_1=X_1\oplus X_2\oplus N_1,\qquad Y_2=X_2\oplus N_2,
\end{equation}
with $N_1\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p_1)$, $N_2\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p_2)$ independent of
each other and of the inputs, $p_1,p_2\in(0,\tfrac12)$. Receiver $i$ decodes
only message $i$; the encoders are independent. This paper records the
complete capacity region of \eqref{eq:model}, in closed form, for all
parameter values --- together with proofs short enough to be taught in a
first course.

The result is significant less for its difficulty than for what it
crystallizes. First, it exhibits the sharpest known contrast between the
binary and Gaussian one-sided interference problems: the binary weak regime
closes in eight lines because Mrs.~Gerber's Lemma~\cite{WZ73} converts
noise-degradation into a converse exactly, whereas the Gaussian analogue
(via the entropy power inequality) famously does not close the
corresponding gap. Second, it gives a complete, extreme answer to the TIN
question for this channel: treating interference as noise with a single
biased input is \emph{exactly} optimal throughout the weak regime --- no
rate splitting, no auxiliary time sharing --- a discrete counterpart to the
TIN-optimality program of~\cite{GNAJ15}. Third, the weak-regime region
coincides, boundary point by boundary point, with the degraded binary
symmetric broadcast channel region of Wyner--Ziv~\cite{WZ73}: the
interfering codeword plays precisely the role of a broadcast ``cloud
center,'' making the BS-ZIC a clean pedagogical bridge between interference
and broadcast theory.

\subsection{Related work}

The strong-interference program originates with Carleial~\cite{Carleial75}
and Sato~\cite{Sato81} and was completed for the discrete memoryless case by
Costa and El~Gamal~\cite{CEG87}; textbook treatments of the one-sided case
appear in El~Gamal and Kim~\cite[Ch.~6]{EGK11}. Our strong-regime result
(Theorem~\ref{thm}(i)) is an instantiation of that machinery. For the weak
regime, Benzel~\cite{Benzel79} determined the capacity region of a class of
discrete additive \emph{degraded} interference channels; Liu and
Ulukus~\cite{LU06} extended the class and its broadcast-channel
correspondence; and Liu and Goldsmith~\cite{LG09} characterized the capacity
region of a class of Z-interference channels containing \eqref{eq:model} in
single-letter (auxiliary-variable) form, observing the capacity-region
equivalence between the Z-channel form and Benzel's two-output additive
model. The closed form of Theorem~\ref{thm}(ii) is thus classical in
substance: our contribution is the explicit assembled statement for all
$(p_1,p_2)$, the direct Mrs.~Gerber converse (which bypasses the
broadcast-channel correspondence entirely), the two-line achievability
identity, and the uniqueness of the sum-optimal point. For the Gaussian
Z-interference channel, by contrast, the weak-interference capacity region
remains open~\cite{Costa85}; the best known results characterize sum
capacity only in restricted regimes.

\section{Model and Main Result}

Throughout, $h$ is the binary entropy function,
$h^{-1}:[0,1]\to[0,\tfrac12]$ its inverse on $[0,\tfrac12]$, and
$a\st b=a(1-b)+b(1-a)$ the binary convolution. Rates are in bits per channel
use; a rate pair is achievable in the standard sense of vanishing average
error probability.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm}
The capacity region of the BS-ZIC \eqref{eq:model} is:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] if $p_1\le p_2$ (strong-to-moderate interference):
\[
\bigl\{(R_1,R_2)\ge 0:\ R_1+R_2\le 1-h(p_1),\ \ R_2\le 1-h(p_2)\bigr\};
\]
\item[(ii)] if $p_1>p_2$ (weak interference), with
$q=\dfrac{p_1-p_2}{1-2p_2}\in(0,\tfrac12)$:
\[
\bigl\{(R_1,R_2)\ge 0:\ R_2\le 1-h(p_2),\ \
R_1\le 1-h\bigl(q\st h^{-1}(h(p_2)+R_2)\bigr)\bigr\}.
\]
In the weak regime the sum capacity is $1-h(p_2)$, attained only at
$(0,\,1-h(p_2))$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

Part (ii) is exactly the capacity region of the degraded binary symmetric
broadcast channel with noise parameters $(p_1,p_2)$~\cite{WZ73}, and its
boundary is traced by treating interference as noise with a biased input for
user~2 and a uniform input for user~1.

\section{Strong-to-Moderate Interference}

\paragraph{Converse} Since $p_1\le p_2$, write $p_2=p_1\st p_0$ with
$p_0\in[0,\tfrac12)$ and couple $N_2^n=N_1^n\oplus N_0^n$, where
$N_0\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p_0)$ is i.i.d.\ and independent of everything else;
this preserves the joint law of $(X_2^n,Y_2^n)$. Data processing then gives
\[
I(X_2^n;Y_2^n)\;\le\;I(X_2^n;X_2^n\oplus N_1^n)\;=\;I(X_2^n;Y_1^n\mid X_1^n),
\]
the identity because, given $X_1^n$, the map $Y_1^n\leftrightarrow
X_2^n\oplus N_1^n$ is a bijection, and the encoders are independent. With
Fano's inequality,
\[
n(R_1{+}R_2)\le I(X_1^n;Y_1^n)+I(X_2^n;Y_1^n\mid X_1^n)+n\epsilon_n
= I(X_1^n,X_2^n;Y_1^n)+n\epsilon_n\le n\bigl(1-h(p_1)\bigr)+n\epsilon_n,
\]
using $H(Y_1^n)\le n$ and $H(Y_1^n\mid X_1^n,X_2^n)=nh(p_1)$; and
$nR_2\le n(1-h(p_2))+n\epsilon_n$ is the point-to-point bound.

\paragraph{Achievability} Take both inputs i.i.d.\ uniform. Receiver~1
jointly decodes both messages over the mod-2 multiple-access channel
$Y_1=(X_1\oplus X_2)\oplus N_1$, which succeeds iff
$R_1,R_2,R_1{+}R_2\le 1-h(p_1)$; receiver~2 requires $R_2\le 1-h(p_2)$.
Since $p_1\le p_2$, the constraint $R_2\le1-h(p_1)$ is implied by
$R_2\le1-h(p_2)$, and the achievable region equals the converse region.
This is the classical strong-interference argument
\cite{Sato81,CEG87,EGK11} instantiated at \eqref{eq:model}.\hfill$\blacksquare$

\section{Weak Interference}

\paragraph{Converse} Now $p_1=p_2\st q$ with
$q=(p_1-p_2)/(1-2p_2)\in(0,\tfrac12)$; couple $N_1^n=N_2^n\oplus Q^n$ with
$Q\sim\mathrm{Bern}(q)$ i.i.d.\ independent, preserving the law of
$(X_1^n,X_2^n,Y_1^n)$. Let $V^n=X_2^n\oplus N_2^n$, which is distributed as
$Y_2^n$. Fano's inequality at receiver~2 gives
\[
\tfrac1nH(V^n)\;\ge\;h(p_2)+R_2-\epsilon_n.
\]
Mrs.~Gerber's Lemma~\cite{WZ73} states that for any binary $V^n$ and
independent i.i.d.\ $Q^n\sim\mathrm{Bern}(q)^n$,
$\tfrac1n H(V^n\oplus Q^n)\ge h\bigl(q\st h^{-1}(\tfrac1n H(V^n))\bigr)$.
Therefore
\[
nR_1\;\le\; n-H(Y_1^n\mid X_1^n)+n\epsilon_n
\;=\;n-H(V^n\oplus Q^n)+n\epsilon_n
\;\le\;n\Bigl[1-h\bigl(q\st h^{-1}(h(p_2)+R_2-\epsilon_n)\bigr)\Bigr]
+n\epsilon_n,
\]
using $H(Y_1^n\mid X_1^n)=H(X_2^n\oplus N_1^n)=H(V^n\oplus Q^n)$
(independent encoders) and the monotonicity of $h(q\st h^{-1}(\cdot))$.
Letting $n\to\infty$ yields the stated bound.

\paragraph{Achievability} Treat interference as noise with a biased input:
let $X_2\sim\mathrm{Bern}(\pi)$ i.i.d.\ carry user~2's message and
$X_1\sim\mathrm{Bern}(\tfrac12)$ user~1's. Receiver~2 achieves
$R_2=I(X_2;Y_2)=h(\pi\st p_2)-h(p_2)$. Receiver~1 sees $Y_1=X_1\oplus Z$
with $Z\sim\mathrm{Bern}(\pi\st p_1)$, so $R_1=I(X_1;Y_1)=1-h(\pi\st p_1)$.
Substituting $s=\pi\st p_2$, so that $h(s)=h(p_2)+R_2$, and using
$p_1=p_2\st q$, the associativity and commutativity of $\st$ give
$\pi\st p_1=(\pi\st p_2)\st q=s\st q$, hence
\[
R_1\;=\;1-h\bigl(q\st h^{-1}(h(p_2)+R_2)\bigr):
\]
the inner bound traces the converse curve exactly as $\pi$ runs over
$[0,\tfrac12]$. Lower rates follow by coding below the two point-to-point
mutual informations.

\paragraph{Uniqueness of the sum-optimal point} Along the boundary,
$R_1+R_2=1-h(p_2)+h(s)-h(q\st s)$ with $s\in[p_2,\tfrac12]$. For
$s<\tfrac12$ and $q>0$ one has $s<q\st s<\tfrac12$, hence
$h(s)<h(q\st s)$ and the sum is strictly below $1-h(p_2)$; equality holds
only at $s=\tfrac12$, i.e.\ at the corner $(0,\,1-h(p_2))$: any sum-optimal
operation silences the interfering user.\hfill$\blacksquare$

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Broadcast equivalence} In the weak regime, receiver~1 observes
user~2's codeword through strictly more noise than receiver~2 does; the
mirror coupling $N_1\overset{d}{=}N_2\oplus\mathrm{Bern}(q)$ makes this a
literal degradation, and user~2's codeword plays the role of the cloud
center in a degraded broadcast channel. Mrs.~Gerber's Lemma is precisely the
tool that makes the Wyner--Ziv broadcast converse work, and it transplants
verbatim. After relabeling, the channel is capacity-region equivalent to
Benzel's discrete additive degraded interference channel~\cite{Benzel79}
(the equivalence is stated by Liu--Goldsmith~\cite{LG09}; Benzel's literal
two-output additive law differs from \eqref{eq:model}), and the channel
satisfies both defining conditions of the Liu--Goldsmith
class~\cite{LG09} exactly and in $n$-letter form --- mod-2 additivity makes
$H(Y_1^n\mid X_1^n{=}x_1^n)$ invariant in $x_1^n$, and the uniform input
maximizes the interfered receiver's output entropy irrespective of the
interference law --- so Theorem~\ref{thm}(ii) can equally be read as a
closed-form evaluation of their auxiliary characterization, with a
degenerate auxiliary.

\paragraph{No rate splitting anywhere} In the strong regime the optimal
scheme decodes the interference completely; in the weak regime it ignores
the interference completely. The Han--Kobayashi partial-decoding machinery,
generically necessary for interference channels, is nowhere needed for
\eqref{eq:model} --- an extreme, exactly provable instance of the
TIN-optimality phenomenon studied in the Gaussian setting in~\cite{GNAJ15}.

\paragraph{The Gaussian contrast} The binary weak regime closes because
Mrs.~Gerber's Lemma gives the exact extremal tradeoff between input entropy
and output entropy across a BSC. The Gaussian analogue of that role is
played by the entropy power inequality, which is not tight for the
interference geometry, and the Gaussian Z-interference channel's weak
regime remains open~\cite{Costa85}. The BS-ZIC thus marks, in the simplest
possible setting, exactly where the discrete theory outruns the Gaussian
one.
